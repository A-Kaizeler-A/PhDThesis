\chapter{Mitochondria, SGs and Inflammation: A Novel Regulatory Triad}
\label{chap:ChapterVI}
Given that senescent cells exhibit cytoplasmic accumulation of mitochondrial \gls{dsrna} that drives inflammatory signalling\cite{lopez-polo_release_2024}, and that \gls{sg}s have been reported to counteract senescence\cite{omer_stress_2018}, we propose the following working model (Figure 9.1). In the absence of \glspl{sg}, cellular stress promotes leakage of mitochondrial \glspl{rna} into the cytosol, where they activate the  \gls{rigi}/\gls{mda5}/\gls{mavs} innate immune pathways\cite{lopez-polo_release_2024}. These pathways canonically detect viral nucleic acids and trigger interferon and inflammatory responses, ultimately promoting \gls{sasp}\cite{dhir_mitochondrial_2018}. Notably, mitochondrial \gls{dsrna} has been shown to activate these same pathways despite its endogenous origin\cite{dhir_mitochondrial_2018}. When \glspl{sg} are present, they may sequester these leaked nucleic acids, potentially explaining the frequently observed proximity of \glspl{sg} to mitochondria\cite{amen_stress_2021, fung_production_2013}, thereby dampening inflammatory signalling and possibly delaying \gls{sasp} onset.
Several recent studies support this model, demonstrating that mitochondrial \gls{dsrna} can localise to \glspl{sg} via increased mitochondrial membrane permeability\cite{krieger_trafficking_2024}, stress-induced release in fibrosis\cite{travers_mitochondria-derived_2025}, or during ageing\cite{rhine_rna_2025}. However, an important caveat remains: both fibrosis and ageing are strongly associated with oxidative stress\cite{antar_fibrosis_2023, liguori_oxidative_2018}, which itself promotes mitochondrial dysfunction and nucleic acid leakage\cite{xu_mitochondria_2025}. It therefore remains unclear whether the proposed mitochondria-\gls{sg}-inflammation axis represents a general stress response, or whether it is specific to conditions that directly compromise mitochondrial integrity. As such, in a collaborative effort, I ``migrated" to Vanessa Morais Lab, where I conducted the experiments below, under support of experts in mitochondria.

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{U-2 OS Growth Conditions}

Human osteosarcoma \gls{u2os} cells were cultured in \gls{dmem} (high glucose) supplemented with 10\% fetal bovine serum. Cells were plated on glass coverslips in 24-well plates and used for all experimental procedures.
\\
\\
Reagents and material references are supplied in section 10.1.1.


\subsection{Stress Conditions}

To examine \gls{mrna} localisation under stress, when 80\% confluency was reached, cells were exposed to 0.4 \gls{molar} sorbitol for 3 hours. To inhibit mitochondrial \gls{dsrna} leakage into the cytosol, 50 \gls{umolar} \gls{dids}(as previously described\cite{krieger_trafficking_2024}) was applied either in combination with sorbitol or alone (control) for the same duration.
\\
\\
Reagents and material references are supplied in section 10.1.1.


\subsection{Immunofluorescence}

Following stress treatments, cells were washed three times with \gls{pbs}+/+ (\gls{pbs} supplemented with calcium and magnesium). Fixation was performed using 4\% formaldehyde in \gls{pbs}+/+ (\gls{ph} 7.4) for 20 minutes at room temperature, followed by three additional washes with \gls{pbs}+/+. Cells were then permeabilised with 0.1\% Triton X-100 in \gls{pbs}+/+ for 10 minutes at room temperature and washed three times with unsupplemented \gls{pbs}.
\\
Glass coverslips were removed from 24-well plates and transferred to a humid chamber containing 20 \gls{ul} droplets of blocking buffer (5\% goat serum in \gls{pbs}). Coverslips were inverted onto the droplets (cell side facing down) and incubated for 60 minutes at room temperature.
\\
Primary antibodies against G3BP1 were diluted 1:200 in blocking buffer and applied using the drop method (10 \gls{ul} per coverslip). Coverslips were incubated for 2 hours at room temperature in the humid chamber, followed by three washes with unsupplemented \gls{pbs}.
\\
Secondary antibodies were diluted 1:500 in blocking buffer together with \gls{dapi} (1:1000) and applied (10 \gls{ul} per coverslip) for 1 hour at room temperature in the dark. Coverslips were then washed three times in unsupplemented \gls{pbs} with agitation (5 minutes per wash).
\\
For mounting, 2 \gls{ul} of Vectashield mounting medium was placed on each microscope slide, and coverslips were positioned cell-side down onto the drops. Coverslips were sealed with nail polish prior to imaging.
\\
\\
For \gls{rna}-\gls{fish} experiments, Stellaris probes targeting three classes of mitochondrial RNA (rRNA, tRNA, and mRNA) were designed, adapted from \cite{begeman_spatial_2025}. Probe hybridisation was performed according to the manufacturer's protocol. Probes against \gls{trna} and \gls{mrna} yielded no detectable fluorescent signal, while the \gls{rna} probe produced signal of insufficient quality for analysis. Multiple protocol variations were tested to optimise signal quality, including sequential \gls{if}-\gls{rna}-\gls{fish} and \gls{rna}-\gls{fish}-\gls{if} combinations using the fixation step of the respective leading protocol. Satisfactory signal for both \gls{if} and \gls{rna}-\gls{fish} was ultimately achieved only through a \gls{rna}-\gls{fish}-\gls{if}-gls{rna}-\gls{fish} sequential protocol, in which Stellaris probes were hybridised twice.
\\
\\
Reagents and material references are supplied in the Key Resources Table.

\subsection{Microscopy and Image Analysis}

For standard microscopy images, after fixation, cells were imaged on a ZEISS \gls{lsm} 880 confocal microscope, using a 63x/1.4 Plan-Apochromat \gls{dic} oil immersion objective. For each condition, a z-stack of a single field of view (1024x1024\gls{px}, with a \gls{px} size of 0.13 \gls{um}) was acquired, comprising 10–20 Z-planes, with a 0.48 \gls{um} step size, according to Nyquist, with a scan zoom of 1.0. G3BP1 was excited using a 633 \gls{nm} laser, \gls{hsp60} with a 561 \gls{nm} laser, \gls{dsrna} J2 with a 488 \gls{nm} laser, and \gls{dapi} with a 405 \gls{nm} laser. Images were observed in \gls{fiji}, with maximum intensity thresholds defined for each channel using sorbitol-stressed samples. These same parameters were then applied to all other conditions to ensure consistency across images. For \gls{rna}-\gls{fish} experiments, acquisition differences were only done in regards to laser excitation, where G3BP1 was excited using a 633 \gls{nm} laser, \gls{rrna} with a 561 \gls{nm} laser, \gls{hsp60} with a 452 \gls{nm} laser, and \gls{dapi} with a 405 \gls{nm} laser.
\\
\\
For hyper-resolution images, previously observed samples in standard microscopy were re-acquired using a ZEISS \gls{lsm} 980 with Airyscan 2 confocal microscope, with a 63x/1.4 Plan-Apochromat \gls{dic} oil immersion objective, using the Airyscan 2 in Super Resolution mode (Airyscan SR). Three fields of view were acquired per condition (2245x2245px, with a px size of 0.035 µm), with each z-stack comprising 35–65 Z-planes with a 0.130 µm step size, according to Nyquist, with a scan zoom of 1.7. G3BP1 was excited using a 639 nm laser, HSP60 with a 561 nm laser, dsRNA J2 with a 488 nm laser, and DAPI with a 405 nm laser.  Raw Airyscan data were processed using Airyscan Joint Deconvolution to improve resolution (Sample structure "Dense" and 5 Maximum Iterations). Basal autofluorescence levels were determined from a control sample incubated with secondary antibodies only and used as a minimal threshold for normalizing fluorescence signals in experimental images.
Colocalisation analysis was performed in Imaris 10.2.0 CF, using the Coloc module, quantifying the percentage of overlapping voxels across different channels. Initially, colocalisation between HSP60 (mitochondria) and J2 (dsRNA) was measured. Negative controls were used to define the minimum intensity threshold for colocalisation of each channel. A new channel was subsequently generated, containing only the J2 signal that did not overlap with HSP60. This non-mitochondrial J2 signal was then analyzed for colocalisation with G3BP1 (stress granules) under both unstressed and sorbitol-stressed conditions.
\\
As signal was of low quality using this acquisition method, RNA-Fish samples were not quantified. 
\\
All image processing and quantifications were performed on an image analysis and processing workstation with support from the Bioimaging Platform of the Gulbenkian Institute for Molecular Medicine Foundation.
\\
Regarding the statistical analysis of the data, colocalisation percentages were initially compared between stressed and unstressed samples using Student's t-tests. However, given the limited sample size, beta distributions were fitted to the three replicates per condition. From these fitted distributions, 1000 random samples were drawn and compared across several statistical metrics, including Cohen's d, and \gls{auc}.
\\
\\
Reagents and material references are supplied in section 10.1.1.


\section{Mitochondrial Permeability is Required for SG Assembly}

To avoid the confounding effects of oxidative stress, which is tightly linked to mitochondrial dysfunction and nucleic acid leakage\cite{xu_mitochondria_2025}, hyperosmotic stress was induced via sorbitol exposure in \gls{u2os} cells, a widely employed model system in \gls{sg} research (reviewed in Figure 4.6). Although hyperosmotic stress can affect mitochondrial function\cite{ikizawa_mitochondria_2023}, it was reasoned that it would perturb mitochondria less directly/extensively than oxidative stress, providing a more controlled context in which to assess \gls{sg}-associated RNA dynamics.
\\
Previous work has demonstrated that inhibition of \gls{bax}/\gls{bak} or the mitochondrial porins \gls{vdac}1/2 prevents cytoplasmic leakage of mitochondrial \gls{dsrna}\cite{krieger_trafficking_2024}. We hypothesised that this mechanism would extend to hyperosmotic stress, and therefore included the \gls{vdac}1/2 inhibitor \gls{dids}\cite{krieger_trafficking_2024} as an additional experimental control. Negative detection of \gls{dsrna} within \glspl{sg} under these conditions would support a mitochondrial origin, as opposed to alternative cytoplasmic sources.
\\
To directly visualise the putative \gls{sg}-mitochondria-inflammation axis, \gls{if} was performed using antibodies against \gls{g3bp1} (\gls{sg} marker; reviewed in Figure 4.6), \gls{hsp60} (mitochondrial marker\cite{el_manaa_transcription-_2021}), and J2 (dsRNA marker\cite{krieger_trafficking_2024}), alongside \gls{dapi} for nuclear counterstaining.
\\
Using conditions established by Matheny \gls{etal} 2021\cite{matheny_rna_2021}, 63x confocal imaging confirmed that \gls{u2os} cells exposed to 0.4 \gls{molar} sorbitol for 3 hours robustly formed \glspl{sg}, visualised as discrete \gls{g3bp1}-positive foci, which were absent in unstressed controls (Figure 9.2). Strikingly, co-treatment with the \gls{vdac} inhibitor \gls{dids} (50 \gls{umolar}) completely abrogated \gls{sg} assembly (Figure 9.2), suggesting that mitochondrial permeability, or at minimum, \gls{vdac} channel activity, is required for \gls{sg} formation under hyperosmotic stress.

This finding is particularly notable given prior reports that \glspl{sg} modulate mitochondrial permeability through regulation of VDAC activity\cite{amen_stress_2021}, and may point towards a reciprocal relationship in which \glspl{sg} both influence and depend on mitochondrial signalling. However, closer inspection revealed that DIDS-treated cells, whether stressed or unstressed , displayed a broadly similar diffuse distribution of G3BP1 (Figure 9.2), raising the possibility of off-target or pleiotropic cellular effects of \gls{dids} that complicate exclusive attribution of the phenotype to \gls{vdac} inhibition.
\\
Nevertheless, the requirement for \gls{vdac} activity is consistent with a model in which mitochondria-derived signals contribute to \gls{sg} assembly. Although leakage of mitochondrial nucleic acids represents a plausible trigger, particularly given VDAC's reported role in their cytoplasmic release\cite{krieger_trafficking_2024}, other mitochondria-associated damage signals, including cytochrome c, cardiolipin, and metabolic intermediates, could also initiate inflammatory or stress responses\cite{lin_mitochondrial-derived_2022} and subsequent \gls{sg} assembly. Impaired mitochondrial import of cytoplasmic factors represents an additional alternative mechanism worthy of consideration.
\\
It should be noted that the J2 antibody is not specific to mitochondrial \gls{dsrna} and could in principle detect \gls{dsrna} from alternative sources, including, for example, transposable element-derived \gls{dsrna} arising from nuclear destabilisation\cite{krieger_trafficking_2024}. To more specifically assess mitochondrial \gls{dsrna}, these experiments were repeated using RNA-FISH targeting a mitochondrially encoded transcript, yielding consistent results in which \gls{dids} treatment blocked or attenuated \gls{sg} formation, though with somewhat more diffuse signal (Figure 9.3).

\section{Mitochondrial dsRNA is Enriched in the SG}

Because \glspl{sg} preferentially form in proximity to mitochondria, as previously reported\cite{amen_stress_2021, fung_production_2013}, conventional confocal imaging did not provide sufficient spatial resolution to directly quantify \gls{dsrna} colocalisation within \glspl{sg}. To overcome this limitation, the same samples were re-imaged using Airyscan with joint deconvolution, which markedly improved spatial resolution. As \gls{dids} treatment altered the baseline distribution of \gls{g3bp1}, comparisons were restricted to stressed versus unstressed conditions only. Additionally, during acquisition trials, it became apparent that this method was not compatible with RNA-FISH samples, as their signal was too sparse, and thus, these were discarded from this analysis as well.
\\
Unexpectedly, at this resolution, \gls{g3bp1} signal appeared as small, discrete foci in both stressed and unstressed cells, rather than the diffuse cytoplasmic distribution typically observed in unstressed cells by conventional confocal microscopy (Figure 9.4A). This complicated the discrimination of bona fide \glspl{sg} from background \gls{g3bp1} signal. Nevertheless, given that these were the same samples as in the preceding analysis (Figure 9.2), quantitative colocalisation measurements were performed.
\\
The fraction of mitochondrial volume colocalising with dsRNA (in voxels) was first calculated (Figure 9.4A, cyan). Counterintuitively, mitochondrial-\gls{dsrna} overlap was greater in stressed than in unstressed cells (Figure 9.4B). This may reflect enhanced dsRNA clearance under hyperosmotic stress via nuclease activity or autophagic pathways, or stress-induced remodelling of mitochondrial morphology that increases measured colocalisation. Alternatively, even under nominally unstressed culture conditions, cells may experience a baseline level of stress insufficient to trigger \gls{sg} assembly yet sufficient to promote mitochondrial dsRNA leakage\footnote{This theory is consistent with the observation that in our unstressed cultures (v. Figure 5.3), TIA1 does not exhibit the expected unstressed pattern of nuclear localisation, but instead a cytoplasmatic distribution, more akin to stressed conditions, albeit no nominal SGs are observed.}. Since \glspl{sg} are thought to exert cytoprotective functions\cite{paget_stress_2023}, their assembly during acute stress might itself reduce such leakage independently of direct nucleic acid uptake. Consistent with this interpretation, evidence from \textit{C. elegans} suggests that inhibition of protein synthesis enhances mitochondrial function\cite{mallick_amp_2025}, and given that protein synthesis is broadly suppressed under acute stress\cite{kedersha_g3bpcaprin1usp10_2016}, this could paradoxically increase mitochondrial activity and \gls{dsrna} production.
\\
To more accurately assess \gls{sg}-associated \gls{dsrna} uptake, \gls{dsrna} signal colocalising with both mitochondria and \glspl{sg} was excluded prior to quantification, isolating \gls{dsrna} of putatively cytoplasmic origin. The proportion of this filtered \gls{dsrna} volume colocalising with \gls{g3bp1} was then measured (Figure 9.4A, magenta). As had been noted for mitochondria, colocalisation of dsRNA within SGs was elevated under stress (Figure 9.4C), being consistent with active dsRNA sequestration into \gls{sg}s. To account for the limited sample size, data were modelled using a beta distribution and the resulting distributions compared (see 9.1.4). Although statistical significance was not achieved owing to limited power, the analysis yielded a large effect size (\gls{auc} = 0.969; Cohen's d = 2.847) (Figure 9.4C), supporting a robust difference between conditions.
\\
Collectively, these observations are consistent with the hypothesis that \glspl{sg} sequester cytoplasmic dsRNA under hyperosmotic stress. Given the technical limitations and modest sample size, however, these data should be regarded as supportive rather than definitive, and further experimental validation will be required to consolidate this model.
