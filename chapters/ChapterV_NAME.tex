\chapter{SGs and Cancer}
\label{chap:ChapterV}

\section{Inflammation: A double agent in plain sight}
Armed with hte knowledge that SGs are anti-inflammatory, it still wasnt clear what impact they would have on cancer. Inflammation itself after all, is one of the most ambigous and controversial effects in cancer. It can be tumour promoting, if this inflammation drives cell death, wich in turn leads to an environment where mutations are more frequent, thus driving cancer evolution and therapy resistance, or it can be tumour supressing, as inflammation molecules act as chemoatractnts for the immune system, that can theoritically attack cancer cells directly. Similar to what was done throughout the thesis, we thus opted to revisit several datasets that directly implicated SGs and cancer, with the goal of discovering if, like inflimation, SGs efffect in cancer was also contxt dependent.

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{TCGA expression data}

Expression data (in FASTQ format) generated by the TCGA Research Network were downloaded from the \gls{gdc} Data Portal\cite{network_cancer_nodate, heath_nci_2021}. 
Read count data for each cancer type were pre-processed separately. We started by filtering out genes deemed uninformative due to their very low expression across samples: only genes with at least one CPM in at least 40\% of the samples were kept for analysis, akin to what we previously described in other large scale datasets\cite{schneider_voyager_2024}.

\subsection{TCGA preprocessing}

Raw reads were trimmed using Trimmomatic (v0.39)\cite{bolger_trimmomatic_2014}. Trimmed reads were then aligned to the \gls{hg38} reference genome using STAR (v2.7.9a)\cite{dobin_star_2013} with the following parameters:

\begin{lstlisting}
	--outFilterType BySJout
	--alignSJoverhangMin 8
	--alignSJDBoverhangMin 1
	--alignIntronMin 20
	--alignIntronMax 1000000
	--alignMatesGapMax 1000000
	--outFilterMultimapNmax 1
	--outFilterMismatchNmax 999
	--outFilterMismatchNoverLmax 0.04
\end{lstlisting}
\noindent
Aligned reads (\gls{bam} files) were subsequently annotated using the GENCODE v42 primary assembly\cite{frankish_gencode_2021} and quantified with featureCounts (v2.0.3)\cite{liao_featurecounts_2014}.

\subsection{DGEA}

To model expression changes between conditions, we fitted linear models to the gene expression data, that accounted for sample type (primary tumour vs normal) and tissue of origin. Standard errors were then moderated using empirical Bayes shrinkage\cite{smyth_linear_nodate}, and differentially expressed genes were defined as indicated on the figures.

\subsection{GSEA}

GSEA\cite{subramanian_gene_2005} was performed using the fgsea (v1.20.0)\cite{korotkevich_fast_2016} R package. Genes were ranked by t-statistics from differential expression analysis, ordered from most positive to most negative, and analysed against the Hallmark Pathways gene sets\cite{liberzon_molecular_2015}. The NES was extracted to quantify the extent to which a given gene set is enriched at the extremes of the ranked gene list. A positive NES indicates that the gene set is enriched amongst over-expressed genes, whereas a negative NES indicates depletion. 

\subsection{Alternative hypothesis testing for SGs implication in cancer}
To explore mechanisms beyond SG assembly that could account for the phenotypes observed in G3BP1 and PTGS2 knockout cells, specifically the effects on survival and drug sensitivity, we leveraged multiple computational resources. PTGS2 perturbagens were  identified using the Connectivity Map134. Drug sensitivity profiles were obtained from the Genomics of Drug Sensitivity in Cancer (GDSC135) database. Survival analyses were performed using GEPIA136, with samples stratified by median expression of each gene.

\subsection{SG signature}

To define an expression signature indicative of SG presence in a given sample using a machine learning approach, we first created a “discovery” dataset by integrating GSE13898834 and GSE11997750,51. This integration allowed comparison of stressed cells with and without SGs while controlling for confounding variables, including cell type, genotype, stressor and study of origin. Batch effects arising from these variables were corrected prior to analysis, following our previously applied methodology122. We then employed an elastic net regression with 1000 bootstrapping iterations with an elastic net mixing parameter (alpha) of 0.75, yielding a more stringent signature. Genes consistently selected in at least 900 iterations were retained. This signature included positive genes (i.e., upregulated in SG positive cells) and negative genes (i.e., downregulated in SG positive cells), thus including bi-directionality. 
SG score for individual samples was calculated using a non-parametric gene ranking method. Within each sample, all genes were ranked by expression. The SG score for a given sample was computed as the sum of the ranks of the genes in the signature, weighted by (i.e. multiplied by) +1 for positive genes or -1 for negative genes, and normalised by the number of genes in the set. This signature was validated on an independent dataset, GSE1739537, with the same batch effect correction as mentioned above122.

\subsection{Survival Analysis}
For survival analysis, GDC-TCGA samples99,100 were stratified into top and bottom quartiles based on SG scores. Kaplan-Meier survival curves were generated and statistical significance was assessed using the log-rank test. To evaluate the significance of observed survival associations, 100 random signatures matching the original SG score signature in size and gene directionality (positive/negative weight) were generated by sampling gene names from the TCGA LAML dataset99,100. This dataset was chosen for its large number of expressed genes after filtering. Survival analyses were repeated for each random signature, and false discovery rates were calculated as the proportion of random signatures with log-rank p-values lower than those obtained from the original SG signatures. 

\section{The problem with single-gene approaches in SG studies:}

\subsection{In metastisation}

Given the established link between inflammation and cancer84, SGs’ involvement in inflammatory regulation may help explain this association. Namely, SGs have been reported to enhance the metastatic potential of sarcoma cells25, based on comparisons between SG competent and SG deficient cells. However, SG ablation in these studies was achieved via G3BP1 knockout, a protein with functions beyond SG assembly, which may have influenced the phenotype. Moreover, G3BP1 knockout cells can still form SGs under certain stressors, such as hyperosmotic stress50, which may occur in the tumour microenvironment. Thus, it remains unclear whether SGs were truly ablated, complicating interpretation of their direct role in metastasis.
Multiple genes, including G3BP185, AGO286, BRF187, CAPRIN188, CPEB189, DDX689, DISC190, EIF2A91, FASTK92, FMR193, FXR193, G3BP285, L1TD194, SAMD4A95, SMN196, TIA197, TIAL197 and ZFP3687, are known to induce spontaneous SG assembly when overexpressed (compiled elsewhere98). We hypothesised that, if SGs directly drive tumour aggressiveness, high expression of these SG-nucleating genes would correlate with poorer patient survival. Analysis of Genomic Data Commons’s The Cancer Genome Atlas (GDC-TCGA)99,100 sarcoma’s dataset revealed that only G3BP1 expression was significantly associated with survival differences (Figure 12), suggesting that its prognostic value likely reflects roles beyond SG assembly rather than a direct effect of SGs.

\subsection{In Chemorresistance}

SGs have also been proposed to promote chemotherapy resistance26. KRAS mutant cells reportedly enhance SG assembly paracrinally via PTGS2 upregulation26. However, PTGS2 has SG-independent roles, and observed resistance may not be solely due to SG assembly. KRAS mutant cells exhibit constitutive activation of the EGFR pathway101. As KRAS functions downstream of EGFR, its mutation predicts poor response to EGFR inhibitors but improved response to MEK inhibitors, which act further downstream102 (Figure 13A-B). PTGS2 KD or inhibition leads to expression changes that correlate with EGFR KD (and ERBB3 KD, another protein from the same family), suggesting that PTGS2 may indeed be part of the same pathway (Figure 13C). Hypothesising that PTGS2 acts downstream of KRAS, its inhibition may mimic MEK inhibition, targeting a key oncogenic driver and explaining increased treatment sensitivity. While PTGS2 is known to be regulated by the EGFR pathway (reviewed in103), this proposed mechanism remains to be experimentally validated.

\section{The SG-Score}

\subsection{Creating a SG transcriptomic signature}

The results above highlight the limitations of single-gene analyses in isolating SG-specific effects due to overlapping roles in other stress-related pathways. To address this, we developed a custom SG transcriptomic signature. First, we integrated datasets from T. Matheny 2019 and 202134,50, as this combination had stressed samples with and without SGs, applying batch effect correction for cell genotype, stressor type, and study of origin. We then used an elastic net regression model with bootstrapping to derive a SG signature (v. Methods). Genes retained in nearly all bootstrap iterations (>90\% of iterations) were used to construct the final signature, comprised of 41 genes, which we dubbed the SG Score. Both were validated on an independent dataset, M. Paget 20247, which had also undergone batch correction for the abovementioned variables, and successfully distinguished SG-positive from SG-negative samples (Figure 14A/B). Nevertheless, it is important to note that the SG Score provides a relative, not binary, measure of SG presence. This means that higher scores indicate a greater likelihood but do not confirm SGs are exclusive to samples with elevated values. 

\subsection{SGs as survival biomarkers}

We next evaluated the performance of our SG signature GDC-TCGA datasets99,100. These datasets include: Acute Myeloid Leukemia (LAML); Adrenocortical Cancer (ACC); Bile Duct Cancer (CHOL); Bladder Cancer (BLCA); Breast Cancer (BRCA); Cervical Cancer (CESC); Colon Cancer (COAD); Endometrioid Cancer (UCEC); Esophageal Cancer (ESCA); Glioblastoma (GBM); Head and Neck Cancer (HNSC); Kidney Chromophobe (KICH); Kidney Clear Cell Carcinoma (KIRC); Kidney Papillary Cell Carcinoma (KIRP); Large B-cell Lymphoma (DLBC); Liver Cancer (LIHC); Lower Grade Glioma (LGG); Lung Adenocarcinoma (LUAD); Lung Squamous Cell Carcinoma (LUSC); Melanoma (SKCM); Mesothelioma (MESO); Ocular melanomas (UVM); Ovarian Cancer (OV); Pancreatic Cancer (PAAD);  Pheochromocytoma and Paraganglioma (PCPG); Prostate Cancer (PRAD); Rectal Cancer (READ); Sarcoma (SARC); Stomach Cancer (STAD); Testicular Cancer (TGCT); Thymoma (THYM); Thyroid Cancer (THCA); Uterine Carcinosarcoma (UCS). 
Given that cancer cells often encounter conditions known to promote SG formation2,23,24, we hypothesised that our SG signature would yield higher scores in tumor samples compared to their normal counterparts. Among the 17 TCGA cancer types with at least 5 normal and tumour samples, only four: BLCA, LIHC, PRAD, and READ, showed an increased SG Score in tumour samples (t-test, p < 0.05). In contrast, seven cancer types: ESCA, HNSC, KIRC, KIRP, LUAD, LUSC, and STAD, displayed lower SG Scores in tumors compared to normal tissue (t-test, p < 0.05), in particular ESCA and STAD (Cohen’s d < -1) (Supplementary Figure 10). 

To assess prognostic relevance, we performed a survival analysis comparing the top and bottom quartiles of SG scores, based on the hypothesis that more aggressive, therapy-resistant cancers would show higher scores, and thus lower overall survival25,26 (Supplementary Figure 11). 
Since random signatures tend to exhibit prognostic value in cancer104,105, we further tested our signatures against randomly generated signatures to assess their specificity (v. Methods; Supplementary Figure 11).  Compared to 100 random signatures, our signature showed a false positive rate (FPR) < 0.05 in four cancer types (ACC, CHOL, MESO, and STAD). This suggests SG presence may relate to tumour aggressiveness in these cancers, as higher SG scores consistently lead to worse outcomes. Importantly, STAD had lower SG scores in tumours than in normal tissue, so its prognostic significance should be interpreted cautiously.

\subsection{How do SGs impact cancer}

To better understand why SGs showed prognostic potential only in these specific cancer types, we compared tumor samples from the four relevant GDC-TCGA cohorts with the remaining GDC-TCGA datasets99,100, while controlling for tissue of origin. This analysis revealed reduced expression of BCL2106 (Figure 14C), suggesting a pro-apoptotic environment106, along with GSEA of downregulated proliferation-associated gene sets and enrichment of epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT) and inflammation-related gene sets (Figure 14D).
Given the proposed role of SGs in attenuating inflammatory signaling7, it was unexpected that higher SG scores correlated with worse survival in cancers characterized by heightened inflammatory pathways. This raises the possibility that, at least in these cancers, this increased inflammation may be boosting anti tumour immunity, thus impairing tumour growth (Figure 14D) (potential mechanisms reviewed elsewhere107).
To further investigate the basis of this association, we compared the top and bottom quartiles of SG Score within each of the four cancer types, while controlling for tissue of origin. DGE analysis identified upregulation of HK2 (Figure 14E), which binds VDAC to promote pore opening108. While this typically facilitates ATP release108, previous results65 (Figure 10A) suggest it could also permit mitochondrial nucleic acid leakage, which we previously linked to SG assembly and inflammation (Figure 9 and 10). Consistent with this, GSEA revealed an apparent “inversion” relative to the basal state (Figure 14F): tumors with higher SG scores showed reduced EMT (previously upregulated) and enhanced proliferation-associated programs (previously downregulated). However, increased interferon-α and -γ responses persisted, seemingly at odds with the idea that SGs suppress inflammation. One possibility is that we are not detecting a net anti-inflammatory effect but rather comparing tumors under stress vs tumors under lesser amounts of stress. In this comparison SGs may mitigate, but not eliminate, stress-induced inflammation. Consistent with this hypothesis of simple mitigation, there is a strong downregulation of EMT, and thus potentially metastization, which has been shown to be reliant on chronic inflammation107.
To test whether survival differences were driven by baseline pathway activity, we repeated survival analyses using the top two enriched basal gene sets (TNFα signaling and EMT). However, neither pathway showed prognostic value in these cancers (TNFα signaling only had FPR < 0.05 in ESCA, and EMT in LGG), suggesting that these results are more particular to our SG Score than to these general processes.
